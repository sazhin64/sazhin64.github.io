</strong>By </strong>Sergey Sazhin

In our modern world things are changing so fast that it is difficult to keep up with the times. In a way, living in a small town in the countryside of a huge country like Russia is a blessing in disguise. We have experienced so many changes, ideological, social, religious changes, that in a way we have developed a certain cynicism for modernization, and we have become cautious to any of those puzzling, challenging influences sweeping the country.

Our small town for us is a city that preserves things of a foregone past that is more attached to family roots than it is to social mores. It is no wonder that we love family gatherings to exchange stories and experiences that only an extended, loving family can provide. When the creeping Russian winter snows us in, there is no TV that can replace the live storytelling of things past, especially from our childhood. I hope I can clearly convey these feelings to my son Volodia, my potential grandchildren and, since my wife Tanya and I are teachers, to our students, too, before they stretch out their wings and fly away lured by the lightings of a big city.

This winter has been very cold. There was some kind of permanence to that sensation snow instills in our souls of Nature showing up how beautifully pervasive She can be.

One of those nights a couple of weeks ago, we gathered to remember things past. Uncle Vladimir was there, making jokes and jovial remarks, my mother Tamara serving tea and cookies (bread sticks, sweets, cakes, sandwiches). My son showing off his brand new computer and how proud he was to have learned in a few days how to configure it.

I couldn’t help telling him, “What about me?” He flashed a big smile on his face, came to me and said, “thanks, Dad!” Then we saw Tanya’s brother Vladimir. I said, “Are you still eating apple halves?” Everybody laughed out loud, but one old friend who didn’t have any idea why everybody was laughing. Sergey demanded an explanation.

I thought it was my duty to tell him about the times when a leftover apple half was as good as the whole thing. Vladimir laughed, “I still miss it!” Vladimir is a nice, tall guy, straight blond hair covering half his forehead. He has a golden heart and a great affection for Tanya.

After the war, the big one, the economic situation in the then Soviet Union was bad for the common people, especially in isolated places. Many things were scarce and food was an expensive commodity. My wife’s parents were going through that difficult period when fruit or sweets were rationed and given to small children. Vladimir used to pick up his little sister at the Kindergarten or pre-school center. Every day he brought his an apple their parents bought for her. She ate the fruit with noisy delight, yet this tiny, weak little girl would not eat the whole apple. Her heart did not allow Tanya to see her brother longing for a piece. She gave him half her apple every day.

The thing is that Vladimir got so used to eat her sister’s apple leftover, that even today when he gives her an apple, he still expects to get her leftovers. It still amazes him to see her sister eat a whole apple.
