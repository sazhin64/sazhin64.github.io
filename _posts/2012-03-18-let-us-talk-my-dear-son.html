</strong>There is so much in life I would like to communicate to you.</em></p>
 

It comes to my mind one of those hot days in July, our hottest month in summer. You were only seven when I took you hiking to show you some of my secret places. We got to a high river bank from where we contemplated the fields, the meadows where the cattle grazed freely, and then, far away. the thick forest.

It was then when I showed you those beautiful places that moved me so deeply when I was still a child. I said to you, “Watch the wild ducks. See the pikes in the river, and the lilies of the field!”

Then I didn’t realize how hard it was for a seven year old to hike 10 miles. I picture him tall, and strong and handsome and brave.

You asked me, “How much further should we go?”

I answered, “You will forget the distance watching the treetops. You will feel so touched, you will cry.”

Down the hills we went and inhaled the fragrance of the flowers, so different one from the other. One fragrance came to us stronger as we passed through. I identified each one of them, “This meadow sweet smell is bedstraw.”  I was sure that by doing so the mental imprint in your child’s memory will remain bright and clear throughout your adult life.

The air was full of different sounds, Billions of grasshoppers, cicadas and black flies. There were zooms, chirps, and buzzes. Birds live their lives in a hurry. They sang roulades around us. Larks circled absentmindedly around us. I wanted to catch them with my eyes, but I was blinded by the sun. An intense emotion pressed my heart and I felt like crying. I heard you shout, “freedom, freedom!” My eyes were filled with moisture. We began to sing a song popular at that time.

Its melody spread afar, yet no one heard it, because we were alone. Our fishing rods swung at the beat of our steps. It was a long road, but singing helped us on our hike. We laughed — we were so happy. Downhill there was a crystal clear water spring. To prevent the cattle from leaving their dirty footprints, herdsmen fenced it with rocks. Thirsty as we were, we welcomed the cool water. We drank slowly, the water was indeed cold.

Yet the sun up on our head made the earth and rocks hot. Every sound joined to another as in a nature’s chorus. I said to you, “Vova, all this is yours. No matter what may happen in your life, here you will always find shelter and food.”

We went on. Our fishing rods were ready for angling. Time stopped yet hours became fleeting. Going back was hard, our energy dwindled, but we had a good helper: Our song. Those happy sounds again and again. “What was really important for us?” We were alive. We had our own house and we had our Motherland — wide and beautiful. I said to you, son, “Never forget home, the place where you were born no matter where you go.”

On our way back home, we met the spring again. We sat on the warm rocks and stretched our legs out for several minutes.  I said, “How are your legs feeling? A bit of fatigue is pleasant; it relaxes you, and unloads the anxieties from your soul. Apprehensions go away with used up energy.”

We closed our eyes and relaxed. Then, I asked you, “Do you hear me? There are so many things I would love to tell you: To have purpose in life, to work hard, to be cheerful and understanding. Always remember how your family and relatives love you. Never be afraid to come back home.”

After our brief rest, the energy came back to our bodies. We stood up and walked until we saw the roofs of the houses in our village.
